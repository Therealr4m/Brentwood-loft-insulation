The Different Types of Loft Insulation Explained

Choosing the right loft insulation for your property is one of the biggest factors in how well your home retains heat during colder months. A lot of homeowners around Brentwood know they need better insulation, but they are not always sure what type of insulation is actually suitable for their loft space.

The reality is there is no single insulation material that works perfectly for every property. The right choice usually depends on the age of the house, the amount of loft space available, the current insulation depth and how the attic is being used.

Around Brentwood we regularly see older properties in CM13, CM14 and CM15 still sitting with outdated insulation depths of around 100mm or less. In some lofts the insulation has also become compressed, uneven or poorly fitted over time which reduces overall thermal performance.

In this guide we’ll explain the different types of loft insulation commonly used in UK properties, where each material works best and what Brentwood homeowners should realistically expect from each option.

Mineral Wool Loft Insulation

Mineral wool insulation is still the most commonly installed loft insulation material across the UK. This is the standard loft roll insulation most people are familiar with and it is widely used because it performs well, is cost-effective and works in most traditional loft spaces.

It is normally installed between and across the loft joists in multiple layers to achieve the recommended 270mm depth. For most standard Brentwood lofts, mineral wool is usually the most practical option when improving heat retention.

Good Thermal Performance

Mineral wool helps reduce heat escaping through the roof and performs well when installed evenly across the loft floor.

Cost Effective

Compared to some other insulation systems, mineral wool is generally more affordable for full loft coverage.

Suitable For Older Homes

Many older semi-detached Brentwood properties are ideal candidates for mineral wool loft insulation upgrades.

One thing that matters with mineral wool is proper installation depth. Simply adding a thin layer of insulation rarely provides the same level of performance as bringing the loft fully up to current standards.

Rigid PIR Loft Insulation Boards

Rigid PIR insulation boards are another type of loft insulation often used where space is more limited or where a higher insulation value is needed within a thinner material.

PIR boards are commonly installed in loft conversions, attic rooms and areas where maintaining head height is important. Unlike loft roll insulation, PIR boards are solid insulation panels which can be fitted between rafters or across roof structures.

In Brentwood we often see PIR boards used in:

  • Loft conversion roof slopes
  • Attic storage areas
  • Modern properties with tighter roof spaces
  • Properties requiring higher thermal efficiency

While PIR insulation performs extremely well thermally, it is usually more expensive than standard mineral wool systems and generally requires more precise installation work.

For some homes, a combination approach works best. We regularly see lofts where mineral wool is used across the loft floor while PIR boards are installed around awkward roof sections or restricted areas.

Loft Insulation Top Ups

Not every property requires a completely new loft insulation installation. In many Brentwood homes the existing insulation is still usable but simply below the recommended depth.

Loft insulation top ups involve adding additional insulation over the current layer to improve thermal performance and bring the loft closer to modern standards.

This is particularly common in homes built during the 1970s, 1980s and 1990s where the original insulation depth may have been considered acceptable at the time but now falls well below current expectations.

Faster Installation

Top up insulation can often be installed more quickly than a complete insulation replacement.

Improves Existing Insulation

Additional insulation layers help improve the overall thermal resistance of the loft.

Suitable For Many Homes

A large number of Brentwood lofts already have insulation installed but simply need the depth improving.

Before topping up insulation it is important to assess the condition of the existing material properly. If the loft insulation has become damp, compressed or contaminated, removal and replacement may sometimes be the better long-term option.

Sheep Wool Loft Insulation

Sheep wool insulation is a more natural insulation material that some homeowners choose for environmental reasons. It is breathable, sustainable and capable of helping regulate moisture within certain roof structures.

Although it is less commonly installed than mineral wool, it can still be suitable in some older properties where breathability is particularly important.

However, the higher material cost usually means sheep wool insulation is not the most common choice for standard loft insulation projects around Brentwood.

For most homeowners focused mainly on heat retention and improving energy efficiency, mineral wool or PIR systems are generally more practical from a cost-to-performance perspective.

What Type Of Loft Insulation Is Best?

The best loft insulation depends on the property itself rather than simply choosing the most expensive material available.

For example:

  • Traditional loft spaces often suit mineral wool insulation best
  • Loft conversions may benefit more from rigid PIR boards
  • Properties with some insulation already installed may only require top ups
  • Restricted roof spaces may require higher-performance materials

Proper installation also matters just as much as the insulation material itself. Uneven coverage, blocked airflow or compressed insulation can reduce performance significantly regardless of which material is used.

Different Types Of Loft Insulation In Brentwood Properties

Across Brentwood we work on a wide mix of property types ranging from older 1930s semis to newer detached developments and converted loft spaces. Because of that, the insulation approach can vary quite a bit from property to property.

In many older homes we still regularly find:

  • Insulation depths below 100mm
  • Large gaps in insulation coverage
  • Old insulation flattened over time
  • Poor airflow around the eaves
  • Mixed insulation materials from previous upgrades

A proper loft assessment helps determine whether the loft needs a complete installation, insulation top up or more specialist insulation materials in certain sections of the attic.
